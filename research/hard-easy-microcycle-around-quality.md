# Hard-easy microcycle around a Quality session — day-before / day-of / day-after design

**Created:** 2026-05-19

---

## TL;DR

1. **Day before a Threshold / VO2max session:** an **easy aerobic
   day** OR a **rest day** is the sport-science consensus — both
   leave the athlete adequately fresh. A multi-day easy block (2–4
   easy aerobic days) BEFORE a Quality reiz is the *classical*
   hard-easy pattern (Seiler 80/20, Daniels) — it does NOT
   contraindicate Quality on the next day; on the contrary, it is
   the canonical setup that protects Quality-session readiness while
   accumulating Z2 volume.
2. **Day of:** Quality lands on the day after a Z2 block when (a)
   HRV/RHR are within or above baseline, (b) TSB is not deeply
   negative (≤ −15 = caution), (c) there is no active red flag in
   `planningConstraints`. Three Z2 days in a row do NOT count as a
   recovery red flag — Z2 is glycogen-sparing and fuels the next
   stimulus rather than depleting it.
3. **Day after:** an **easy run / recovery jog / cross-training easy
   ride / rest day** — never a second Quality back-to-back. The
   spacing matrix (TrainingPeaks): Threshold→Threshold 48–72 h,
   Threshold→VO2max 24–36 h, VO2max→Threshold 6–12 h.
4. **Trade-off "easy run vs. easy bike vs. rest day BEFORE quality":**
   - **Easy run** = lowest organisational overhead, keeps running
     volume up. Cost: orthopaedic load (Achilles, knees).
   - **Easy bike** = full mechanical relief for the lower-limb
     impact chain, blood-flow / leg-priming benefit, often "feels
     better next day" than full rest (Cycling Weekly).
   - **Rest day** = best for full glycogen restoration after a deep
     glycogen-depleting prior session; comparable freshness for the
     next Quality. Active recovery slightly slows glycogen
     replenishment (Sport Journal review) — relevant only when prior
     session was glycogen-depleting (≥ ~75 min Threshold or longer
     Long Run).
5. **Decision driver in the rolling 7-day window:** the **next-Quality
   spacing** matters more than the "easy or rest" question on the
   day before. Pushing Quality one day later means the spacing to the
   next Quality (or Long Run) shrinks by one day. If the next
   Long-Run / Quality is on the weekend and is itself a hard
   stimulus (race-specific Long Run with elevation), keeping ≥ 96 h
   between Quality sessions is safer than the 72 h minimum.

---

## Question / Trigger

A common real-application question: after a 3-day easy Z2 block on
consecutive days, does it make sport-scientific sense to put a
Threshold session on day 4, OR is it better to insert an easy bike
day on day 4 and shift Threshold one day later?

The question matters because it pits two reasonable patterns
against each other:

- **Option A** (Quality on day 4): classical hard-easy with a
  multi-easy-day priming → Quality → easy/rest → easy → Long Run.
- **Option B** (easy bike on day 4, Quality on day 5): one more
  recovery day before Quality, but the spacing to the weekend Long
  Run shrinks accordingly.

Six concrete questions:

1. What does sport-science say about the day BEFORE a Threshold —
   easy aerobic, rest, or no clear preference?
2. What does it say about the day AFTER — minimum spacing to the
   next Quality?
3. Does a 3-day easy block before a Quality cause "too much
   freshness" or under-priming?
4. Is glycogen still a limiting factor after a 3-day Z2 block (Z2 =
   primarily fat oxidation, glycogen-sparing)?
5. Is an easy bike day a stronger or weaker pre-Quality choice than
   an easy run day or a rest day?
6. How does the rolling-7-day spacing constraint (Quality → next
   Quality / Long Run) influence the day-shift decision?

## Findings

### 1. Day before a Quality session — sport-science consensus

Both coach-literature and peer-reviewed sources converge:

> "A threshold run should be **preceded by an easy run or a rest
> day**, which allows you to go into your threshold run feeling
> fresh and recover from it properly." — Marathon Handbook
> ([Threshold Training Guide](https://marathonhandbook.com/threshold-training/))

> "Recovery rides enhance the gains you accumulate from your intense
> sessions, and simultaneously prepare you for the next hard
> workout." — TrainerRoad ([Polarized Training](https://www.trainerroad.com/blog/polarized-training-for-cyclists-what-it-is-and-how-to-try-it/))

> "The three quality training sessions are the most important, with
> hopefully **one day of rest or easy running in between each** to
> allow the body to recover." — Daniels' Running Formula
> ([Review on Sweat Elite](https://articles.sweatelite.co/understand-the-jack-daniels-running-formula-in-15mins/))

**Three takeaways:**

- Either "easy" or "rest" works. Neither is intrinsically superior
  for the day before.
- Multiple easy aerobic days before a Quality is the CLASSICAL
  Seiler-80/20 / polarized pattern — not a caution flag.
- Daniels' template puts Q1 + Q2 (+ Q3) with `E`-days between, not
  with rest-only days. The Q-day after multiple E-days is exactly
  the prescribed pattern.

### 2. Day after a Quality session — minimum spacing

Recovery matrix from TrainingPeaks ([Are You Recovering
Adequately](https://www.trainingpeaks.com/blog/are-you-recovering-adequately-between-high-intensity-workouts/)):

| Last session | Threshold next | VO2max next | Anaerobic next |
|--------------|----------------|-------------|----------------|
| Threshold | **48–72 h** | 24–36 h | 6–12 h |
| VO2max | 6–12 h | **36–48 h** | 24–36 h |
| Anaerobic | 36–48 h | 24–36 h | **24–36 h** |

The "Threshold→Threshold = 48–72 h" applies to comparable Quality
intensities. A Long Run *with elevation / race-specific Quality
intent* counts in the upper half of that range; a pure Long Slow
Distance Long Run counts in the lower half.

**Practical implication for the day after a Threshold:**
- Easy aerobic run, easy bike, or rest day — all valid.
- Do NOT stack two Threshold-class sessions back-to-back.
- If the next Quality lands at 72 h, accept the lower bound; if it
  lands at 48 h, the prior Quality must have been short / lower
  volume to justify the tight spacing (Magness, Daniels).

### 3. Multi-easy-day block before Quality — pre-priming or under-priming?

Three or more consecutive easy aerobic days before a Quality is
**NOT** a sign of under-priming or "lost form." On the contrary:

- **Glycogen status:** Z2 runs operate ~70 % from fat oxidation
  (Sport Journal review). Three Z2 days do not deplete glycogen —
  they keep it topped up while the legs do the long-term aerobic
  adaptation work.
- **Neuromuscular readiness:** the typical complaint after a long
  easy block is "legs feel a bit sluggish at the start of the next
  Quality" — addressed by **2–4 strides in the warm-up**, which
  belongs in the standard pre-Quality warm-up anyway.
- **HRV/RHR readiness:** if HRV is within or above baseline after
  the easy block, the cardiac-autonomic system has cleared the prior
  load — green light for Quality.

The "freshness" concern from a multi-easy-day block is real only
when the block becomes a deload week (5+ days low intensity AND
volume drop AND no strides AND tapering signals). A 3-day
easy-aerobic block at normal volume + standard strides is a
priming block, not a deload.

### 4. Glycogen as a limiter

Glycogen replenishment after a normal training session: **~24 h
with adequate carb intake**, up to **3 days** only if the prior
session was deeply depleting (long fasted training, Long Run >
90 min, multiple consecutive Quality sessions).
([Sport Journal review](https://thesportjournal.org/article/glycogen-replenishment-after-exhaustive-exercise/))

Three Z2 days are not deeply depleting. After three Z2 days the
glycogen tank is **at or above** the level before the block.
Glycogen is therefore NOT the limiter on the day after — readiness
question collapses to HRV/RHR/TSB + perceived fatigue.

Side note from the same body of evidence: active recovery (light
jogging, easy cycling) **slightly slows** glycogen replenishment
compared to full rest. Relevant only after a deeply depleting
session — not after a Z2 block.

### 5. Easy run vs. easy bike vs. rest day BEFORE Quality

| Modality | Mechanical load on the chain | Blood-flow / priming | Glycogen replenishment | When preferred |
|----------|------------------------------|----------------------|------------------------|----------------|
| Easy run | Moderate (impact, eccentric stress) | High (specific) | Slight slowdown | Default — keeps weekly run volume up |
| Easy bike | Very low (no impact) | High (non-specific) | Slight slowdown | When orthopaedic load is the limiter (Achilles, knee, plantar) |
| Rest day | Zero | Zero | Fastest replenishment | When prior session was deeply depleting OR HRV/RHR signal recovery debt |

> "If you have an important interval session coming up, it might
> be best to do a small recovery spin the day before rather than a
> rest day so you feel good from the get-go in that session." —
> Cycling Weekly ([Recovery Ride vs Rest Day](https://www.cyclingweekly.com/fitness/ask-a-cycling-coach-whats-the-point-of-going-out-on-an-easy-recovery-ride))

**For runners with an Achilles / lower-limb history:** an easy
bike day is the *better* pre-Quality choice on weeks where
running-volume capacity is the limiter — it gives the impact chain
24 h relief while keeping the cardiac priming. Easy run is fine
when no orthopaedic flag is active.

### 6. Rolling 7-day spacing as the dominant constraint

When deciding between "Quality today" vs. "Quality tomorrow", the
deciding factor is rarely the day-before recovery — both options
satisfy the day-before requirement. The deciding factor is
**spacing to the NEXT Quality** in the week.

Worked example for a weekly structure with a weekend Long Run that
is itself race-specific (Threshold-class intensity):

- **Quality Tuesday + Long Run Saturday** → spacing **96 h**, well
  above the 72-h Threshold→Threshold minimum.
- **Quality Wednesday + Long Run Saturday** → spacing **72 h**,
  exactly at the minimum.
- **Quality Wednesday + Long Run Sunday** → spacing **96 h**, back
  in the comfort zone.

The rule of thumb: when the weekend stimulus is itself hard,
prefer the EARLY-week Quality slot to keep ≥ 96 h spacing. When
the weekend stimulus is a true Long Slow Distance, the
Quality-mid-week pattern at 72 h spacing is fine.

## Application in framework

### What is changed / refined

1. **`framework/agents/planner.md`** — new sub-rule under "Weekly
   outlook / Hard-Reize-Strategy":
   - When deciding between "Quality today" vs. "Quality tomorrow"
     and HRV/TSB/readiness are green, the deciding factor is the
     SPACING to the next planned Quality (or race-specific Long
     Run) — not the day-before recovery preference. Prefer ≥ 96 h
     between consecutive Quality stimuli when the second one is
     itself hard.
   - A multi-easy-day (2–4 days) block BEFORE a Quality is the
     classical hard-easy pattern, not under-priming. Do not delay
     the Quality "to get fresher" if HRV/TSB are already green.

2. **`framework/agents/specialist-endurance.md`** — wellness
   correction section already covers TSB / HRV — no edit needed.
   New note that "3+ days of Z2 before Quality is not a
   contra-indication" is added inline to prevent silent
   conservatism (CLAUDE.md §No silent conservatism already
   references the canonical anti-pattern).

3. **Wrapper `config/training_paradigms.md` (athlete-specific)** —
   when the athlete's weekly strategy already encodes a fixed
   2-Hard-Reize-Strategy (Reiz 1 + Reiz 2 with a recovery / volume
   buffer in between), this research doc complements it by
   clarifying the day-by-day structure WITHIN that buffer.

### What stays unchanged

- Existing recovery-week trigger logic
  ([recovery-week-triggers.md](recovery-week-triggers.md)) remains
  authoritative for the bigger "block of Easy / Recovery week"
  decisions.
- Plyometrics 48–72 h recovery rule
  ([plyometrics-frequency-recovery.md](plyometrics-frequency-recovery.md))
  is independent — Plyo and Quality-Run are different stress

- Compliance / decoupling triggers
  ([compliance-decoupling-thresholds.md](compliance-decoupling-thresholds.md))

  separate from the day-before / day-after question.

## Open questions / Caveats

1. **Race-specific Long Run intensity classification:** a Long Run
   "with elevation" can land anywhere on the spectrum from Z2 Long
   Slow Distance (low cost) to race-specific tempo (Threshold-class
   cost). The 96-h vs. 72-h spacing rule needs the athlete to
   self-classify the planned Long Run before the spacing decision is
   final.
2. **Masters-athlete recovery scaling:** the cited recovery matrices
   are aggregated from "trained endurance athletes" without explicit
   age stratification. Masters 40+ may need the upper bound of each
   range (e.g. Threshold→Threshold 72 h rather than 48 h). HRV /
   subjective recovery should override the table when in tension.
3. **Multi-easy-block longer than 4 days:** this document only
   covers 2–4 easy days before Quality. Blocks longer than 4 days
   start to overlap with deload-week semantics
   ([recovery-week-triggers.md](recovery-week-triggers.md)) — the
   "freshness" concern becomes valid again at that length, and a
   ramp-back / activator stride session before the next Quality is
   advisable.
4. **Pure aerobic-bike Quality day:** when an athlete uses a Bike
   VO2max session (e.g. Rønnestad 30/15) as the week's second hard
   stimulus, the run-Quality spacing logic must include bike-quality
   in the count. A wrapper's 2-Hard-Reize-Strategy typically already
   encodes this; this caveat just makes it explicit.
