# Running posture — pelvic tilt, hip extension, and the "hips forward / run tall" cue without lumbar hyperextension

**Erstellt:** 2026-07-06

## TL;DR

"Running tall" with hip extension is a **hip-joint** movement driven by
the gluteus maximus, with the pelvis held in or near neutral by
posterior-chain + deep-core (TVA/obliques) co-activation. It is **not**
"push the pelvis forward" — that cue is a well-documented compensation
route into **anterior pelvic tilt + lumbar hyperextension (lordosis)**
when hip extension range is limited (typically by tight hip flexors /
iliopsoas and weak glutes). Biomechanically, runners with reduced peak
hip extension at terminal stance show **more** anterior pelvic tilt, and
lumbar lordosis rises with running speed. The evidence-based cueing
package is: (a) **run tall / grow through the crown**, (b) **whole-body
lean from the ankles**, not from the waist and not by driving the pelvis
forward, (c) posterior-pelvic-tilt / low-abdominal engagement to hold
the pelvis neutral, (d) glute-driven push-off. The corrective package
addresses the underlying cause: hip-flexor mobility + glute strength +
deep-core endurance (McGill-style neutral-spine work). Excessive forward
lean past ~4–5° is metabolically costly and pushes runners back into
the compensation loop.

## Findings

### 1. Neutral vs. anterior vs. posterior pelvic tilt during running

- **Anterior pelvic tilt is the norm during running, not the exception.**
  Multiple 3D-motion-capture studies report the pelvis sits in mild
  anterior tilt throughout the gait cycle, with **peak anterior tilt at
  or immediately after toe-off** (early flight phase), coincident with
  peak hip extension. Typical peak values in healthy runners sit in the
  range ~10–15° anterior tilt at 3–4 m/s, higher at faster speeds.
- **Lumbar lordosis scales with running speed.** Reported means rise
  from ~26° (walking) to ~33° (running) — a real increase in lumbar
  extension load that anchors why running form matters for LBP-prone
  athletes.
- **Neutral pelvis is a coaching target, not a kinematic constant.**
  The goal is not zero tilt; it is **stability of tilt** across the
  gait cycle (small oscillation) at a value close to the athlete's
  standing neutral — not a large excursion into deeper anterior tilt at
  toe-off.

### 2. The mechanism: reduced hip extension → compensatory anterior tilt + lumbar hyperextension

The single most-cited biomechanical loop for the "hollow-back runner":

1. **Hip-flexor tightness / anterior-capsule stiffness** (iliopsoas,
   rectus femoris) limits **passive** hip extension.
2. To achieve the stride length the runner still wants, the pelvis
   **rotates anteriorly** at toe-off — the femur no longer travels
   behind the pelvis, the pelvis rotates forward around the femur.
3. Anterior pelvic tilt is mechanically coupled to **lumbar
   extension** (the sacrum tips forward, dragging the lumbar spine into
   deeper lordosis). This is the "Hohlkreuz-runner" pattern.
4. In that position, the glutes lose mechanical advantage (they are
   optimally leveraged at a neutral / slightly posteriorly-tilted
   pelvis). Extension work shifts onto **hamstrings** (predisposition
   to hamstring strain) and **lumbar erectors** (LBP load).
5. Schache et al. (2000, BJSM) demonstrated the coupling **directly**:
   runners with reduced peak hip-joint extension at terminal stance
   show **greater** anterior pelvic tilt — the pelvis is doing the work
   the hip joint isn't.
6. Crucially, Schache also showed the **static** modified-Thomas test
   is a poor predictor of the **dynamic** pattern. Testing static
   flexibility alone underestimates the problem; the run itself is
   the assessment.

**Adding to the loop on the muscular side:** weak / inhibited **gluteus
maximus** cannot drive hip extension actively; the athlete recruits the
lumbar erectors and hamstrings to substitute. Weak **deep core** (TVA,
internal obliques) fails to hold the anterior pelvis down against the
iliopsoas pull at toe-off. Both compound the tilt.

### 3. Distinguishing hip extension (good) from lumbar extension / anterior tilt (bad)

| Movement | Joint | Muscle driver | Coaching goal |
|----------|-------|---------------|---------------|
| Hip extension | Hip (femur behind pelvis) | Gluteus maximus, hamstrings | **Increase** — 10–15° at toe-off is efficient |
| Anterior pelvic tilt | Pelvis-on-femur / lumbosacral | Iliopsoas (dragging), lumbar erectors, hip-flexor tightness | **Minimise excursion** — keep near standing-neutral |
| Lumbar extension | Lumbar spine | Erector spinae, tight hip flexors (indirect) | **Avoid dynamic hyperextension** — hold neutral with deep core |

The "hips forward" cue conflates all three. Athletes hear "push the
pelvis forward" and, if hip extension range is limited, achieve the
**illusion** of forward drive by tilting the pelvis anteriorly and
hyperextending the lumbar spine — the exact opposite of the intended
mechanic. The efficient version is: **femur travels behind a stable
pelvis**, not: **pelvis travels forward of the femur**.

### 4. Evidence-based cueing — what produces the good version

Cues that reliably orient runners into a tall, neutral-pelvis, glute-
driven posture without the lordotic compensation:

1. **"Run tall — grow through the crown."** Vertical cue, not a
   horizontal push. Directs attention to spinal extension via axial
   elongation, not lumbar hyperextension.
2. **Whole-body forward lean from the ankles, not from the waist / not
   from the pelvis.** The body is a straight, rigid segment tilted
   forward a few degrees at the ankle. Hinging at the waist collapses
   the chest and dumps the glutes; **driving the pelvis forward**
   produces anterior tilt. The neutral cue is "one straight line, tilted
   at the ankle." Carson et al. (2024, PLOS ONE) measured this: moderate
   ankle-lean of ~4° increased stance-phase gluteus-maximus activation
   by ~31% vs. upright; **larger** lean (~8°) increased metabolic cost
   ~8% and became inefficient. Sweet spot is small.
3. **Slight posterior-tilt / "tuck" cue as a corrective bias**, not as a
   held position. For runners who habitually sit in anterior tilt,
   cueing "belt buckle up toward the ribs" or "low abs on" during the
   run pulls the pelvis toward neutral without over-tucking. This is a
   **counter-cue** — held only long enough to reset the pattern, then
   released back to neutral.
4. **Glute-driven push-off.** Attentional focus on "squeezing the glute
   as the leg goes behind you" recruits the correct extensor. Effective
   in retraining after the deep-core + hip-flexor mobility work has
   restored the range.
5. **Deep-core bracing (TVA / low-abdominal engagement), not
   six-pack-crunching.** Neutral-spine isometric endurance is the
   protective pattern — the same class of core work covered in
   [lumbar-core-stabilization-mcgill-big3.md](lumbar-core-stabilization-mcgill-big3.md).
   Loaded lumbar flexion (sit-up, crunch) is not the intervention; low-
   load neutral endurance is.

Anti-cues to remove from the vocabulary of a runner with LBP-tendency
or anterior-tilt pattern:

- **"Push the hips forward"** — reliably produces anterior tilt when
  hip extension is limited.
- **"Chest up" / "chest out" / "shoulders back" without a tall cue** —
  often lands as thoracic extension driven by lumbar hyperextension.
- **"Big stride" / "lengthen from behind"** — encourages pelvis-forward
  compensation to fake range the hip joint doesn't have.

### 5. Corrective package (strength / mobility) — addressing the underlying cause

Cueing alone doesn't fix the biomechanical loop when the range and
strength aren't there. The evidence-supported combination:

- **Hip-flexor mobility** — iliopsoas + rectus femoris length work
  (couch stretch, half-kneel hip-flexor stretch), specifically to
  restore **passive** hip extension range. Stretching studies (e.g.
  Konrad-style protocols) show pelvis returns toward neutral after
  hip-flexor length is restored.
- **Gluteus maximus strengthening** — bridges, hip thrusts, split-
  squats with intentional glute drive at lockout. Restores the correct
  extensor; reduces hamstring/lumbar-erector substitution.
- **Deep-core / neutral-spine isometric endurance** — McGill Big-3
  (modified curl-up, side plank, bird dog) — targets TVA + obliques +
  QL for pelvic stability without loading lumbar flexion. See
  [lumbar-core-stabilization-mcgill-big3.md](lumbar-core-stabilization-mcgill-big3.md).
- **Optional: draw-in maneuver + manual-resistance protocols** have
  been shown to increase gluteus-maximus activity and reduce erector-
  spinae activity during hip extension — a rehab-context adjunct, not
  a running cue.

### 6. Practical translation for a runner with lumbar-stability focus / anterior-tilt tendency

Priority order when the pattern is present:

1. **Diagnose the loop, not just the symptom.** LBP after runs +
   visible lordosis on video + tight hip flexors on modified-Thomas =
   the classic loop, even if the athlete "runs tall" subjectively.
2. **Cue in this order during running:** *tall spine → low-ab
   engagement (soft) → ankle lean → glute squeeze at push-off*.
   Explicitly drop any "hips forward" cue.
3. **Address the range and strength off the run:** daily hip-flexor
   mobility, 2–3×/week glute strength, daily / near-daily neutral-
   spine core endurance.
4. **Verify on video, not on feel.** The anterior-tilt pattern is
   proprioceptively invisible to most athletes — they feel "tall" in a
   deep lordosis. A side-on video at running speed is the honest read.
5. **Progress running volume and speed only as the pattern holds.**
   Lordosis increases with speed; rushing into faster / longer sessions
   before the neutral-pelvis pattern is durable re-loads the lumbar
   spine.

## Open questions / Caveats

- **Cue-response individuality.** The evidence supports the cue
  package as a class of interventions; the individually most-effective
  cue (external focus "tall" vs. internal focus "glute squeeze" vs.
  tactile "belt buckle up") varies by athlete and by training age.
  Video-based iteration is required.
- **How much anterior tilt is "too much"?** Peak anterior tilt at
  toe-off > ~20° combined with visible lumbar lordosis / LBP correlates
  with the compensation pattern in the literature, but no clean
  clinical cutoff exists. The signal is pattern + symptom, not a
  single degree number.
- **Speed dependence.** Lumbar lordosis increases with running speed
  in every dataset. For athletes with an active LBP-tendency, the
  neutral-pelvis pattern must be demonstrated at **race speed**, not
  only at easy-Z2 — the compensation reappears when the athlete is
  pushed.
- **Fatigue dependence.** Fatigue amplifies anterior tilt and lumbar
  lordosis (2025 Frontiers data). Video assessment in the fresh state
  underestimates the pattern; a fatigued-state clip (end of a long
  run or late in an interval session) is more diagnostic.
- **Coach-source vs. primary-source split.** The cue **wording** is
  practitioner-derived (Kinetic Revolution, Balanced Runner,
  Microcosm-style coaching). The **mechanism** and the **direction**
  of the intervention (increase hip-joint extension range, hold pelvis
  neutral, drive with glutes, avoid pelvis-forward cue) is primary-
  literature-supported. Do not attribute mechanism claims to the coach
  sources.
