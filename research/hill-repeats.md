# Hill repeats — gradient, duration, intensity, typology (run)

**Created:** 2026-05-16

---

## TL;DR

1. **Hill repeats have three clearly distinguishable sub-formats** with different gradient × duration × intensity:
   - **Hill sprints:** 7%+ gradient, 5-10s, all-out — neuromuscular / speed-power
   - **Hill repeats (short):** 4-7% gradient, 30-90s, ≥3K-pace effort — VO2max stimulus + strength
   - **Hill reps (long):** 2-5% gradient, 2-4 min, 5K-10K-pace effort — threshold/VO2max + endurance power
2. **Uphill HIIT with moderate gradient (8%, 4×5min, isoeffort, 90s passive) produces ~42% more time-above-90% VO2max** than the flat variant at the same RPE, the same HR and the same lactate (Steinmetz et al. 2023). Mechanism: less elastic recovery → more concentric muscle work → higher O2 uptake.
3. **But:** a 6-week intervention in trained distance runners shows that *level-grade* intervals deliver more performance gain on a direct time-to-exhaustion endpoint than uphill (Ferley et al. 2013). → Uphill is **complementary / impact-sparing**, not a replacement for classical tempo stimuli on the track.
4. **Standard anchor for 4×4min uphill VO2max stimulus in the framework:** 6-8% gradient, isoeffort (i.e. as hard as the athlete can hold across 4 reps), HR target mid-Z4 to top of Z4, 90-120s passive or easy walking rest downhill, 10-15 min WU + CD.

---

## Question / Trigger

Hill repeats are mentioned in the framework — Strava insights, trail preparation, specialist-endurance templates — but:

- No evidence-based parametrisation (which gradient for which purpose?)
- No documented intensity anchor (pace? HR? %vVO2max?)
- No clear relation to the classical VO2max long-interval studies (Helgerud 4×4min) and the newer uphill-HIIT research
- No comparison to hill sprints (10s neuromuscular) vs. hill reps (4 min VO2max) — often conflated

Four concrete questions:

1. Which gradient ranges have which physiological focus?
2. How does time-above-90% VO2max compare uphill HIIT vs. flat HIIT?
3. Which performance endpoints (vVO2max, Tmax, race pace) improve through uphill-focused programs — and which benefit *less*?
4. Which intensity anchor is sensible when uphill pace is not directly comparable with track tempo due to topography?

## Findings

### 1. Gradient typology (gradient × duration × stimulus)

From the coach literature (TriTrainingHarder, Coach Parry, RunnersConnect — consistently cross-cited) a three-part model can be extracted:

| Format | Gradient | Duration | Intensity anchor | Stimulus |
|--------|----------|----------|------------------|----------|
| Hill sprints | ≥7% (often 8-12%) | 5-10s | all-out | Neuromuscular, maximal strength, top-end speed |
| Hill repeats (short) | 4-7% | 30-90s | 3K-pace effort / mid-Z4 to Z5 HR | VO2max, run strength, power |
| Hill reps (long) | 2-5% | 2-4 min | 5K-10K-pace effort / Z4 HR | Threshold + aerobic power, posterior-chain endurance |

**Mechanics:** steep gradients (≥7%) extend ground-contact time, load the quads strongly and require dry-mass strength → short stimulus, otherwise hamstring/Achilles overload. Flat gradients (2-5%) load the posterior chain (glute, hamstring) over longer activation duration and are the only variant that makes a pace anchor in the 5K range actually sustainable (Coach Alan / TriTrainingHarder: "longer contact time on a shallower gradient places less load on your quadriceps").

### 2. Uphill HIIT vs. flat HIIT — time-above-90% VO2max

**Steinmetz et al. 2023 (Frontiers in Physiology, n=17, well-trained, VO2max 63.3±4.2):** crossover test, 4×5min isoeffort, 90s passive between reps. Compared 1% (horizontal) vs. 8% (uphill).

> "uphill HIIT revealed higher … accumulated time ≥90% VO2max (uphill: 9.1 ± 4.6 vs. horizontal: 6.4 ± 4.0 min; SMD = 0.62)" — corresponds to **+42% time-above-90% VO2max** at the same RPE, same HR and same lactate.

Mechanistic explanation by the authors: "the use of elastic energy may be compromised, so that in turn more mechanical energy (i.e., greater concentric muscle activity) needs to be generated" — uphill the tendon-energy return becomes weaker, the athlete must produce more active concentric work, which raises O2 demand.

Practical implication of the authors: over a 6-week intervention the gain in time-above-90% VO2max would be **~30 minutes additional** to the flat variant — equivalent to 5 additional flat sessions.

**Source:** Steinmetz, Bauer, Schega et al. (2023). "Increased oxygen uptake in well-trained runners during uphill high intensity running intervals: A randomized crossover testing." Frontiers in Physiology. → [Frontiers DOI](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2023.1117314/full)

### 3. Performance endpoints — uphill is *complementary*, not a replacement

**Ferley et al. 2013 (Journal of Strength & Conditioning Research, n=32, well-trained distance runners, 6 weeks, 12 interval + 12 continuous sessions):** three arms — uphill HIIT, level HIIT, control. Endpoint: Tmax (time-to-exhaustion at vVO2max).

> "both uphill and level-grade interval training can induce significant improvements in a run-to-exhaustion test in well-trained runners at the speed associated with VO2max but that traditional level-grade training produces greater gains."

The authors warn that uphill stimuli are metabolically stronger, but for **specific running speed** (i.e. race-pace adaptation) flat stimulation accomplishes more. Explanation: uphill speeds are typically 15-30% below race pace — the neuromuscular speed stimulus is missing.

**Source:** Ferley DD, Osborn RW, Vukovich MD (2013). "The effects of uphill vs. level-grade high-intensity interval training on VO2max, Vmax, V(LT), and Tmax in well-trained distance runners." JSCR. → [PubMed 22996027](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22996027/)

**Derivation for the athlete:**
- If the season goal is a trail / hilly race (e.g. a forest-trail race, mountain-region HM with elevation profile) → uphill stimuli are **race-specific** and should be part of the build
- If the season goal is a flat 10K / half-marathon on asphalt → uphill stimuli remain **a complement** for power + impact sparing, the backbone stays flat tempo stimuli

### 4. Steeper hill group + maximal velocity (Nature 2025)

**Mateu-Ramirez et al. 2025 (Scientific Reports, RCT in middle-distance runners):** comparison of various gradient groups over multiple weeks. Finding: the 7.6% gradient group significantly outperformed the control group in maximal velocity, time-trial, and strength-endurance.

→ For **400-1500m distances** moderate uphill (7-8%) actually delivers speed-endpoint improvements too, presumably via the strength component and neuromuscular recruitment.

**Source:** "The effects of uphill training on the maximal velocity and performance of middle-distance runners: a randomized controlled trial." Scientific Reports. → [Nature SciRep](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08275-w)

### 5. Intensity anchor — why HR + RPE instead of pace

Uphill pace cannot be directly compared with track pace (an 8% gradient costs about 4-5%/% gradient in pace, so at 8% one structurally runs ~30% slower than on flat at the same effort). Recommendation from the coach literature (Magness, Daniels, TriTrainingHarder) consistent:

- **HR anchor:** Z4-mid to top of Z4 on long reps (2-4 min), Z5 entry on short reps (30-90s). The Frontiers study showed: HR is almost identical between uphill and horizontal at the same RPE → HR is a valid cross-format anchor.
- **RPE anchor:** isoeffort across all reps (Steinmetz protocol). Don't force pace.
- **Pace anchor (secondary):** "10-15s/km slower than 5K-PR pace on long reps; near 5K pace on short reps" (Coach Parry).

## Open questions / Caveats

1. **Downhill section separately:** this research only covers uphill. Downhill stimuli have their own physiological logic (eccentric, DOMS, quad damage) and are documented separately (planned M12 — trail tapering).

2. **Age-adapted transfer (masters):** all three primary studies were conducted on well-trained distance runners aged 22-35 y. Transfer to masters 40+ is deductive — HR anchors stay the same, the volume ceiling is more conservative (especially at steeper gradients + Achilles history, where hill sprints in the first 3 months of phase 3 should be avoided).

3. **Treadmill gradient vs. outdoor gradient:** treadmills often have hard caps at 12-15% — higher gradients are only possible outdoors. Treadmill calibration also sometimes deviates from the digitally displayed gradient. For hill sprints (≥7%) outdoor is preferred.

4. **Trail-surface effect not isolated:** the studies cited here run on treadmill or asphalt uphill. Real trail hill reps (gravel, roots, technical footing) have additional neuromuscular load (stabilisation, proprioception) and are not directly comparable with treadmill studies.

5. **Race-specificity derivation:** the recommendation "flat race = complement only, hilly race = primary" is a pragmatic synthesis of the study situation, not directly derivable from a single study.
