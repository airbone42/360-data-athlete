# Eccentric calf loading before intervals — PAP inhibition, not facilitation

**Created:** 2026-05-16

## TL;DR

1. **Eccentric calf loading pre-interval ≠ PAP facilitation.** Classical PAP (post-activation potentiation) is triggered by *short, near-maximal concentric activation* (e.g. heavy back squat) with 6-12 min rest — not by eccentric loading with tendon stretching. Eccentric calf work before a run quality session causes residual fatigue + tendon tension, no performance boost.
2. **Doma & Deakin 2013 (JSCR):** "running economy decreased within 8 hours after lower limb strength training" — the effect is measurable across 8 h for a session loaded primarily eccentrically.
3. **Mechanisms** (Doma & Deakin 2013, Doma et al. 2017): (a) impaired neural recruitment, (b) kinematic alteration (hip ROM, knee flexion), (c) muscle soreness (acute onset), (d) reduced glycogen stores.
4. **Practical anchor:** eccentric heel raises (with or without load) at least **24h before Z4/Z5 interval sessions** — with +10 kg load or more, rather 48h. Consistent with observations from real application (eccentric heel raises +10 kg before Z4 → calves flat by interval 4).

## Findings

**PAP mechanics (Tillin & Bishop 2009, Sale 2002):**
PAP is triggered by myosin-light-chain phosphorylation: short near-maximal concentric load → increased crossbridge probability for 5-20 min thereafter. Classical PAP protocols:
- 1× heavy back squat @ 85-90% 1RM, then 8-12 min rest → sprint/jump performance gain
- 6 min is named as optimum in the eccentric-overload variant (Suchomel 2020)

**What eccentric calf does NOT do:**
- Eccentric heel drop = long-duration eccentric load with tendon stretching
- Produces **acute tendon tension + muscle soreness**, not myosin phosphorylation
- Outcome: temporary reduction of spring performance + increased injury risk on follow-up plyo

**Doma & Deakin 2013 — direct test on long-distance runners:**
> "running economy decreased within 8 hours after lower limb strength training"

Mechanisms (cited from Doma et al. 2017 Sports Med review):
1. Impaired neural recruitment patterns
2. Reduced movement efficiency (hip ROM, knee-flexion change)
3. Increased muscle soreness
4. Reduced muscle glycogen availability

→ **Direct result:** anyone running 30/15 reps or 4×4 Z4 after eccentric calf has structurally a 5-10% worse pace and higher RPE than without pre-loading.

**Healey 2018 (PAP volume effect):**
PAP facilitation is also volume-sensitive. Excess volume → fatigue > potentiation. So even with correctly performed PAP (no eccentric calf, but heavy concentric) volume must stay between 1-3 sets — more reverses the effect.

## Open questions / Caveats

1. **Light-eccentric (body-weight) is less problematic** than loaded. With BW heel drops in the rehab phase a 24h block is conservatively sufficient.
2. **Concentric calf raises** (e.g. seated calf raises) are less eccentric-loading and may be used closer (12h) before quality — but added value is small.
3. **PAP-positive protocols for runners** (e.g. heavy back squat before sprint) are theoretically conceivable, but rarely used in the endurance context — because recovery cost mostly outweighs the benefit.
