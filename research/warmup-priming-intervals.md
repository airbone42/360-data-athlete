# Warm-up priming before VO2max / Threshold intervals — short heavy spikes before the first work rep

**Erstellt:** 2026-06-19

---

## TL;DR

1. **A pure low-intensity warm-up does NOT prime VO2 kinetics for the first interval.** Without progressive heavy spikes, the first work rep is run under a *slow* primary-VO2 response — the athlete spends the first 1–2 reps "catching up" to VO2max instead of accumulating time-at-target. Add **2–3 short heavy-domain spikes (priming bouts) at the end of the warm-up** to accelerate the primary-phase VO2 response, raise its amplitude, and reduce the slow component during the work set.

2. **Evidence-based priming protocol (generic, format-agnostic):**
   - Easy build: **10–15 min Z1–Z2** (active aerobic warm-up).
   - Heavy spikes: **2–3 × 60–90 s in the heavy domain** (cycling: ~90–100% FTP / ~85% MAP; running: ~CV/threshold pace, i.e. slightly faster than easy, distinctly slower than VO2max pace). Recovery between spikes: **60–90 s easy**.
   - Optional neuromuscular touch: **3–5 × 10–15 s** brisk (cycling sprint at moderate cadence, running stride at ~mile-to-3k pace) — primes motor-unit recruitment without anaerobic spill.
   - **Critical rest between priming and the first work rep: ≈ 3–10 min easy**. Below ~3 min → residual fatigue/H+/PCr depletion outweighs the kinetic benefit. Beyond ~12–15 min → the VO2-kinetic priming effect decays.

3. **Differentiation by session class:**
   - **VO2max short intervals (30/15, 30/30):** priming spikes are MANDATORY. Without them the first 2–4 reps fall below the time-above-90%-VO2max window — and that window is the entire stimulus.
   - **VO2max long intervals (4×4, 5×3 min):** priming is RECOMMENDED. The slow primary phase costs less of a long rep, but priming still raises mean VO2 in rep 1 and improves rep-to-rep consistency.
   - **Threshold (Z4, 2×20 / 4×8 etc.):** priming helps but is OPTIONAL. The work intensity is below VO2max — the slow-component effect matters more than the primary-phase amplitude. One submaximal 2–3 min build into threshold pace at the end of the warm-up is sufficient; explicit short heavy spikes are not required.
   - **Easy / Recovery:** NO priming. Spikes would defeat the recovery purpose. Strides at the **end** of an easy run remain a separate neuromuscular-priming tool aimed at *the next quality day*, not at the current easy session (see `strides-protocol.md`).

## Findings

### 1. Why a pure easy warm-up is insufficient before VO2max work

At the onset of any exercise above the gas-exchange threshold (GET), pulmonary VO2 follows three phases: a fast cardiodynamic phase (~15–20 s), a primary phase with a time constant τ ≈ 25–45 s in trained athletes (longer in untrained), and — above critical power — an additional slow component that drives VO2 further upward over minutes. From "cold" (only easy warm-up), the primary phase begins with a smaller amplitude and the athlete reaches VO2max **later** in rep 1. For 30/15 / 30/30 formats where each work rep lasts only 30 s, that delay means rep 1 (and often rep 2) sits below the **time-above-90%-VO2max** band — which is the entire purpose of the protocol.

**Priming exercise** — a heavy-domain bout performed before the main set — accelerates the primary-phase τ and/or raises its amplitude, so the same work rate produces a faster and higher VO2 response in the subsequent set.

> "Prior heavy exercise increased the amplitude of the primary VO2 response and reduced the VO2 slow component trajectory." (Bailey et al. 2009, summarising the consensus from multiple Burnley/Jones studies.)

> "VO2 priming consists of heavy intensity exercise prior to the main training session, with the priming work thought to accelerate the VO2 response during subsequent exercise, thereby increasing athlete's time to exhaustion for efforts above FTP." (Caen et al. 2023 review.)

### 2. Optimal priming intensity — heavy domain, NOT severe

The seminal Bailey/Vanhatalo/Wilkerson/DiMenna/Jones 2009 study (n=8, cycle ergometer, 80%Δ severe-intensity tolerance test) factorially tested **two priming intensities × three recovery durations**:

- 40%Δ (≈ upper heavy domain, below CP) — at recovery durations of 3, 9, 20 min.
- 70%Δ (≈ severe domain, above CP) — at recovery durations of 3, 9, 20 min.

> "Overall VO2 kinetics were accelerated relative to control in all conditions except for 40-9-80 and 40-20-80 conditions … as a consequence of a reduction in the VO2 slow component."

**Translation:** higher-intensity priming (70%Δ — well into the severe domain) accelerates VO2 kinetics across the tested recovery windows, but at the cost of **residual fatigue** at short recovery (3 min). The optimum reported by the study is **severe-intensity priming + ≥ 9 min recovery**: the priming benefit is preserved while PCr is sufficiently restored and blood [H+] has fallen back.

**Why short heavy spikes work for athletes:** in practical warm-up structure, 2–3 short bouts of ~60–90 s at the heavy/severe boundary (heart-rate hits Z3 upper / lower Z4, RPE 6–7) reproduce the priming stimulus without dropping the athlete into the deep anaerobic well. One single 6-min heavy bout (the classical lab protocol) is too long for an athlete already focused on a hard main set.

> "Prior high-intensity exercise can enhance tolerance to subsequent high-intensity exercise provided that it is coupled with adequate recovery duration of at least 9 minutes, which presumably optimizes the balance between preserving the effects of prior exercise on VO2 kinetics and providing sufficient time for muscle homeostasis such as muscle phosphocreatine and H+ concentrations to be restored." (Bailey 2009.)

### 3. Optimal recovery between priming and first work rep

Across the priming literature (Burnley & Jones reviews, Caen 2023), the practical recovery window is bounded:

- **Lower bound (~3 min):** Below this, [H+] and PCr depletion from the priming bout dominate — the athlete enters rep 1 fatigued. The priming benefit is masked.
- **Upper bound (~12–15 min):** Beyond this, the kinetic priming effect on the primary phase decays (re-equilibration of muscle blood flow, gradual re-cooling of metabolic enzymes).
- **Practical sweet spot: 3–10 min easy spinning/jogging.** This matches the cycling-coach consensus:

> "The majority of studies looking at the use of 'priming' within a warm-up have used a priming effort lasting 6-minutes with around 10-20 minutes of recovery between this priming effort and the start of competition or an intensive interval training session." (Caen 2023 / Burnley & Jones synthesis.)

For **short** spikes (60–90 s heavy bouts, not the 6-min lab variant), shorter recoveries are appropriate — the cumulative metabolic load is smaller. **3–5 min after the last spike** is a defensible default; longer (8–10 min) when the spike was harder/longer.

### 4. Concrete protocols by session class

#### 4a. Cycling — VO2max short intervals (Rønnestad 30/15, Billat 30/30, intensified short)

```
10–15 min Z1–Z2 easy spin (HR drifts up to mid-Z2, RPE 2–3)
2–3 × 60–90 s @ ~90–100% FTP (≈ 85% MAP, RPE 6–7, "controlled hard")
   recovery: 60–90 s easy spin between spikes
[optional] 3 × 10–15 s sprint @ 110–130% FTP (open the legs, controlled cadence)
   recovery: 60 s easy spin between sprints
3–5 min easy spin into main set
→ Main set: Rønnestad 30/15 @ MAP-anchored watt target
```

Source: Coach Ben Delaney (TrainingPeaks) for the format-specific Rønnestad warm-up: "a warm-up of 15–20 minutes including 3 × 1-minute openers at FTP is recommended." Source Endurance Cycling for the additional neuromuscular spike component.

#### 4b. Cycling — VO2max long intervals (4×4, 5×3, 4×5 min)

```
12–15 min Z1–Z2 easy spin
2 × 90 s @ ~95–100% FTP (heavy, just sub-VO2max), recovery 90 s easy
5 min easy spin into main set
→ Main set: 4×4 min @ Z5 / ~110% FTP
```

The long-rep format is more tolerant of a "cold" first rep (the slow primary phase costs only the first ~60 s of a 4-min rep), but priming still raises mean VO2 in rep 1 and tightens rep-to-rep consistency.

#### 4c. Running — VO2max intervals (vVO2max reps, 30/30, 5×3 min, Billat)

```
10–15 min easy jog (Z1–Z2)
[dynamic mobility 3–5 min — A-skips, leg swings, optional, see warm-up-drill rule]
2 × 60–90 s @ Critical-Velocity / threshold pace (heavy, RPE 6–7)
   recovery: 90 s easy jog
4 × 20–30 s strides at ~mile-to-3k pace, full standing/walking recovery between
3–5 min easy jog into main set
→ Main set: vVO2max reps
```

**Note on strides as priming:** strides at ~mile-to-3k pace (NOT all-out) double as the neuromuscular-priming layer (motor-unit recruitment, gait-cycle efficiency) — they are NOT redundant with the heavy spikes, they target a different mechanism (neuromuscular, not VO2 kinetic). Strides at the END of an easy run are a separate tool aimed at the next quality day (see `strides-protocol.md`); strides INSIDE a quality warm-up serve the current session.

#### 4d. Running — Threshold (CV / Z4 tempo, 2×20, 4×8 min)

```
12–15 min easy jog (Z1–Z2)
1 × 2–3 min progressive build from easy into threshold pace (the last 30 s should touch threshold)
3–5 min easy jog into main set
→ Main set: Threshold reps
```

Explicit short heavy spikes are NOT required here — at sub-VO2max work intensity, the primary-phase amplitude is reached comfortably within the first rep. A single submaximal build into threshold pace at the end of the warm-up suffices to "open" the metabolic gate. Adding strides is OPTIONAL (helps gait, costs little).

#### 4e. Easy / Recovery runs and rides

NO priming spikes. The session goal is sub-threshold aerobic accumulation or recovery — heavy spikes contradict the stimulus. The existing easy-run HR-ceiling rule (below Z3, see `framework/agents/specialist-endurance.md`) applies unchanged.

### 5. Mechanism — what priming actually does

Three mechanisms are documented; they overlap in the heavy-domain priming window:

1. **VO2 on-kinetics — primary-phase τ shortening and amplitude raising.** The dominant effect. Prior heavy exercise pre-activates oxidative enzymes (mitochondrial Ca²⁺ signalling, PDH activation), raises muscle blood flow / O2 delivery, and shifts the metabolic substrate state toward oxidative phosphorylation. Result: rep 1 of the main set reaches a higher VO2 faster.
2. **Slow-component attenuation.** In severe-domain main efforts the VO2 slow component drives premature exhaustion. Priming reduces the slow-component trajectory — measurable as a *reduced* VO2 drift across reps and a longer time-to-exhaustion at a fixed work rate.
3. **Motor-unit recruitment / PAP-adjacent.** Short brisk spikes (sprints, strides) increase neuromuscular excitability and motor-unit recruitment. This is the same family as a post-activation effect — distinct from the metabolic kinetic effect, additive when both layers are included.

> "Priming exercise performed at heavy or severe intensities can improve exercise tolerance during severe-intensity exercise, with these positive alterations attributed, at least in part, to enhancement of the overall VO2 kinetics." (Caen et al. 2023.)

### 6. Effect-size context

Bailey 2009 reports tolerance improvements of ~10–25% in severe-intensity time-to-exhaustion for the best priming/recovery combinations (severe-intensity priming + 9–20 min recovery). For VO2max-short-interval formats — where the stimulus is **time-above-90%-VO2max** — the relevant proxy is rep-1 VO2 amplitude: priming closes the gap such that rep 1 contributes to the time-at-target rather than being a "ramp" rep. Practical implication for a 30/15 set: with priming, the effective time-at-VO2max window opens at rep 1 instead of rep 3 — a meaningful chunk of the total stimulus.

## Open questions / Caveats

1. **Recovery-window precision for short spikes.** The 3–10 min "rest between last spike and first work rep" range comes from extrapolating the 6-min/9-min lab-priming literature to shorter 60–90 s spikes. No direct study has factorially tested 60-s priming + variable recovery. Practical guidance: shorter spike → shorter recovery (3–5 min); longer spike → longer recovery (8–10 min). If athlete-specific telemetry (rep-1 vs. rep-3 VO2 / HR amplitude) is available, individualise.
2. **Effect-size of priming in masters athletes specifically.** All cited studies are on young trained subjects (typical range 20–35 y). Masters athletes (40+) have slightly slower baseline VO2 kinetics — priming likely matters *more* for them, but a direct study is lacking. Application remains deductive.
3. **Cross-sport HR differential during priming.** When priming a Ride session with Run-derived HR zones, watch for the cross-sport HR offset (see `cross-sport-hr-differential.md`). Anchor priming intensity on watt/%FTP for the bike, on pace/RPE for the run — HR is the sanity cap, not the primary anchor.
4. **Interaction with Compliance-check rule.** When a previous session of the same format had compliance < 95% or decoupling > 10% (see `vo2max-short-intervals.md` §3), the cleaner *next* attempt is to (a) reduce intensity/volume of the main set AND (b) add or strengthen the priming warm-up, not just one of those. Priming is not a substitute for correcting an over-prescribed main set.
5. **Race-day openers (day before) vs. in-warm-up priming.** These are distinct: race-day openers (45–60 min light spin with 3 × 30 s sprints + short threshold touches, the day before the event) target glycogen/neural readiness across 12–18 h. In-warm-up priming targets VO2-kinetic readiness across 3–10 min. Both can coexist; do not conflate them.
