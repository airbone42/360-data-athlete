# Masters-athlete protein requirements — daily dose, per-meal, timing

**Created:** 2026-05-16

## TL;DR

- **Daily dose:** 1.6–2.0 g/kg/d — endurance-focused masters (40–55 y) sit well at 1.7–1.8 g/kg; the upper end (2.0 g/kg) counts as a conservative safety margin and is plausible for trained athletes, but not mandatory
- **Per meal:** 0.4 g/kg (example calculation: ≈ 32 g for an 80 kg athlete); older muscle requires ≥ 2 g leucine per meal (≈ 20–25 g whey) to trigger MPS; higher doses increase the buffer against anabolic resistance
- **Inter-meal spacing:** the "MPS refractory time" of ~3–5 h is real — muscle-full effect (Mitchell et al. 2017, Areta et al. 2013). A rigid 3.5 h rule is not a myth, but no magic window: 3–4 meals at ~0.4 g/kg each, evenly distributed, is the operative goal
- **Timing:** post-endurance 0.4–0.5 g/kg protein (+ CHO for glycogen resynthesis) within ≤ 2 h; pre-sleep protein (casein/quark ≈ 30–40 g) as an optional further stimulus
- **Source robustness:** core studies are strength-focused and often short-term. Endurance-specific masters data are thin; the Moore 2021 review is currently the most robust synthesis

## Findings

### 1. Anabolic resistance in masters athletes

Sedentary older adults develop pronounced anabolic resistance — muscle responds less to amino acids and insulin than younger muscle (Moore 2021). **Trained masters athletes form an exception:** the training stimulus maintains muscle sensitivity to amino acids. Moore (2021) summarises: master athletes are "Just Older Versions of Their Younger Selves" with respect to protein metabolism, as long as training is consistently maintained.

Practical implication: for actively training masters athletes (< 60 y, training continuously), protein recommendations for young athletes are a lower bound, not an upper bound.

### 2. Recommended daily dose (g/kg)

| Population group | Recommendation | Source |
|------------------|----------------|--------|
| Young athletes (general) | 1.4–1.7 g/kg/d | ISSN consensus |
| Master endurance athletes | 1.7–1.8 g/kg/d | Moore 2021 (GSSI) |
| Master strength athletes (>60 y) | 1.8–2.0 g/kg/d | Deutz et al. 2017 |
| Conservative safety margin | ≤ 2.0 g/kg/d | Moore 2021 |

Deutz et al. (2017) in a consensus statement: "Older adults should consume 1.0–1.2 g protein/kg BW/day" (inactive seniors) and "physically active older adults should aim for 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day" — athletic populations clearly higher. The 2.0 g/kg figure for > 60-year-old athletes (strength-focused) has recently been moderated by Moore 2021 to ~1.8 g/kg (endurance).

### 3. Per-meal dose and leucine threshold

**Young athletes:** ~0.24–0.3 g/kg per meal (example: ≈ 19–24 g for 80 kg) maximally stimulates MPS (Moore et al. 2009: linear increase 5→20 g, plateau at ~20 g).

**Older athletes:** the threshold shifts to ~0.4 g/kg (example: ≈ 32 g for 80 kg), justified by:
- Leucine threshold: ≥ 2 g leucine needed to sufficiently activate mTORC1 (Churchward-Venne et al. 2012; Witard et al. 2016)
- ~40 g of protein typically contains ≥ 3 g leucine → solid buffer for masters

Churchward-Venne et al. (2012): a suboptimal protein dose (6.25 g) can be raised by leucine supplementation to the level of a full 25 g dose — but only at rest; post-exercise requires full protein doses.

Paddon-Jones and Rasmussen (2009, J Am Diet Assoc): ~30 g protein per meal is the operative recommendation for older adults; larger quantities are oxidised, smaller quantities stimulate MPS suboptimally.

### 4. Inter-meal spacing and MPS refractory time

**The refractory time is real, not mythology.**

Areta et al. (2013, J Physiol) tested three feeding patterns after resistance exercise (80 g whey in 12 h):
- PULSE: 8 × 10 g every 1.5 h
- INTERMEDIATE (INT): 4 × 20 g every 3 h ← **winner: +31–48% MPS vs. the others**
- BOLUS: 2 × 40 g every 6 h

INT was significantly better. The interpretation: MPS has a "muscle-full" effect — after ~1.5–3 h of amino-acid exposure, MPS returns to baseline, even when plasma amino acids remain elevated.

Mitchell et al. (2017, Clinical Science) confirmed: MPS kinetics return to baseline 3–4 h after a protein meal, while mTORC1 signals remain elevated (dissociation signal ≠ synthesis rate).

**Practical conclusion:** 3–4 meals at ~0.4 g/kg (≈ 30–40 g), ~3–4 h apart, is the evidence-based strategy. A rigid gap of exactly 3.5 h is no magic number — but meals following too close (< 2 h) do not exploit the refractory window. Meals spaced too far apart (> 6 h) miss MPS-stimulation windows.

### 5. Protein sources: Whey vs. Casein vs. Plant

**Whey:**
- Rapidly absorbed, high leucine content (~10–11 g BCAA/100 g protein), strong acute MPS peak
- Preferred source post-exercise (Witard et al. 2016: whey > casein for acute postprandial MPS response)
- 25–40 g post-workout optimal for masters

**Casein:**
- Slowly absorbed, sustained amino-acid release
- Before sleep: casein or quark (80% casein share) ≈ 30–40 g, 1–2 h before sleep → stimulates overnight MPS without sleep interruption
- Less effective for immediate post-exercise MPS than whey

**Plant protein:**
- Typically lower leucine content, restricted amino-acid profile (except soy)
- Pea protein ≈ whey at adequate dose (Banaszek et al. 2019: equal hypertrophy at dose-matching)
- For masters: higher doses required (~30–40% more) to achieve the same leucine delivery
- Combinations (e.g. rice + peas) can provide a complete profile

### 6. Timing around training

**Post-endurance exercise** (key window):
- 0.4–0.5 g/kg protein + 0.8–1.2 g/kg CHO within ≤ 2 h post-exercise
- Endurance increases amino-acid oxidation during the load → more protein needed than strength-only (Moore 2021: up to 0.5 g/kg post-endurance)
- Whey + casein or a whole meal (meat/fish + carbohydrates) equivalent at adequate dose

**Pre-sleep protein:**
- Trommelen and van Loon (2016): 40 g casein 30 min before sleep improves overnight MPS and muscle build-up; replicated in several studies
- Practical: quark (500 g low-fat quark ≈ 50 g protein) or casein shake

**Pre-training:**
- If ≥ 3 h since last meal: 0.4 g/kg protein sensible to limit catabolism
- No acute benefit to immediately pre-exercise protein if post-exercise is secured

### 7. Endurance-specific data vs. strength literature

**Critical caveat:** the bulk of the masters-protein literature comes from resistance-training contexts (strength sport, hypertrophy paradigms). Endurance-specific masters data are markedly thinner.

Differences endurance vs. strength:
- Endurance: higher protein-oxidation risk during training → higher need per session
- Endurance: myofibrillar protein-turnover rate lower, but mitochondrial protein synthesis more important
- Masters endurance athletes: Moore (2021) recommends 1.7–1.8 g/kg/d (endurance) vs. ~1.6 g/kg/d (strength) — i.e. slightly higher for endurance
- Endurance-specific per-meal data are almost entirely missing for the masters population

## Application in framework

**What is confirmed:**
- Daily targets at the upper end of the range (≈ 2.0 g/kg/d) are a **plausible conservative safety margin** — for actively training masters endurance athletes the core recommendation is 1.7–1.8 g/kg; the upper end is a solid buffer, not an error
- Packet size at the upper end of the 0.4 g/kg threshold (example: 0.4 g/kg × 80 kg = 32 g as minimum; ~40 g provides a leucine buffer for masters) is well-justified
- Inter-meal spacing in the ~3–4 h range: **evidence-based correct**; not magic, but works — corresponds to the Areta-2013 INT optimum of ~3 h and lets the refractory time fully run out

**What could be added:**
- Explicitly raise post-endurance protein dose to 0.4–0.5 g/kg (instead of a blanket packet size)
- Include pre-sleep casein as an explicit option (quark) in the protein workflow
- Endurance-specific distinction: after a long session (> 90 min) needs additional protein vs. a short session

## Open questions / Caveats

- **Endurance-specific masters data thin:** most master-protein studies are strength-focused (resistance training, short intervention). Long-term endurance-masters cohorts are almost entirely absent
- **MPS-refractory-time nuances:** the ~3–4 h pause reliably prevents suboptimal stimulation, but the exact timing varies with protein dose, source, and training status. "Exactly 3.5 h" is a practical heuristic, not a biological absolute
- **Sex differences:** women (oestrogen status, menopause) may show stronger anabolic resistance; the data for masters female athletes are even sparser than for men
- **Protein-quality benchmark:** the leucine figures come mostly from whey studies; transferability to mixed whole-food meals (lower leucine bioavailability) is not fully quantified
- **Oxidative loss in endurance:** on very long sessions (> 3 h) amino-acid oxidation rises sharply — absolute protein targets for ultramarathon or > 4h bike days are not specifically evidence-based for masters
